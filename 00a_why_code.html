PROLOGUE · THE HONEST QUESTION

"AI already writes the code — why am I here?"

You have probably tried it: paste a homework prompt into ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude, and out comes a complete, runnable script — NumPy arrays, a Matplotlib figure, even a t-test at the end. It looks like scientific programming has been fully outsourced. So what is left to learn?

The short answer first: precisely because AI can write code, people who can read and judge code have become more valuable, not less. That sounds like a paradox; the next few minutes explain why — and it is the shared view of many senior engineers, including Martin Fowler, one of the most influential thinkers in software engineering.

💡 THINK FOR 30 SECONDS FIRST

An AI hands you a simulation. It runs without errors and produces a beautiful plot — but the number it reports is wrong. How would you ever notice? And once you noticed, could you fix it? Keep your answer in mind and compare it with what you read below.

PART 01 · TWO WAYS OF USING AI

Vibe coding vs. software engineering

People who code with AI fall into two camps. The difference is not whether they use AI — both camps do — but whether they understand what they produce:

🎲 Vibe coding🔧 Software engineering
Attitude to the codeNever reads it — only cares whether it runsUnderstands every line and why it is there
When output is wrongCan only "re-roll the dice" and hopeCan locate the bug and say "this line is wrong — fix it"
Skill over timeStagnates — the AI does the thinkingCompounds with every use
Good forQuick throwaway experimentsAnything you must trust or maintain — like research results

Vibe coding is not evil — for a quick toy or a one-off exploration it is fast and fun. The trouble starts when it becomes your only mode:

📣 MARTIN FOWLER

"It's suitable for exploration and throwaway work, but you wouldn't want to use it for anything that needs long-term maintenance."

This course exists to move you into the right-hand column. Not to make you reject AI — but to give you the competence to read it, judge it, and say "this part is wrong — change it." In scientific computing that competence is not optional: a silently wrong number can sink an experiment, a report, or a paper.

PART 02 · AN UNCOMFORTABLE FACT

AI is confidently wrong

Traditional programs have a beautiful property: determinism — the same input always yields the same output, so errors can be traced step by step. AI assistants are different: ask the same question twice and you may get two different answers, and the wrong one sounds exactly as confident as the right one.

Deterministic (traditional code)Non-deterministic (AI assistants)
BehaviorSame input → always the same outputSame prompt → possibly different answers
TrustTraceable, debuggable, verifiableCan hallucinate — with total confidence
Your roleAuthor of the logicVerifier and gatekeeper
🏗 THE SAFETY-FACTOR MINDSET

A structural engineer never designs a beam to hold exactly the expected load — they add a safety factor against variability in the material. Treat AI-generated code the same way: never accept "the AI says it works." Add your own margin — tests and verification. A test is the contract between you and the AI. (adapted from Martin Fowler's structural-engineering analogy)

Sound familiar? It is exactly the predict → run → verify habit this course drills into you from Module 1 onwards (remember it when you meet the quizzes: predict the answer before you click). In science the stakes are higher than in most software: the most dangerous bug is not the one that crashes, but the semantic error — code that runs happily and returns a plausible, wrong number.

PART 03 · THE LEARNING LOOP

Why the learning loop cannot be skipped

Learning anything follows one loop: idea → try it → observe the result → understand why. Programming is no exception. The biggest cost of pure vibe coding is that it cuts the loop entirely: you supply the idea, the AI does everything, and you ship the result without even looking at it.

📣 MARTIN FOWLER

"When you vibe-code, you actually remove something very important: the learning loop."

"If you don't look at the output, you are not learning."

Picture two people in a kitchen. The professional chef can glance at a recipe and adapt it — less salt, lower heat — because they understand why each step exists. The recipe-follower is helpless the moment the dish changes. Rely on AI without understanding its output, and you are training yourself to be the recipe-follower — and one day you will meet a problem the AI cannot fix, and discover you never actually learned to cook.

💡 THE GOOD NEWS

The same AI, used differently, becomes the best training partner you have ever had. In the next chapter (00B · Environment setup) you will meet Colab's Learn Mode — a mode that deliberately does not hand you finished code, but guides you step by step instead. Used this way, AI spins your learning loop faster instead of cutting it.

PART 04 · SO WHAT?

Why this matters to you — three reasons for a science/engineering student

Everything above was the software engineer's view. Maybe you never plan to be one — fine. Here are three reasons this course is still a good investment, whatever field you end up in:

REASON 01 · Your field already runs on this stack — and someone must be able to judge the code

Physics labs, bio pipelines, financial models, machine-learning papers — NumPy, Matplotlib, SciPy and Pandas are the lingua franca of quantitative work in essentially every discipline. You will work with AI on this stack; the dividing line is who directs whom. A person who cannot multiply cannot get the right answer out of the world's best calculator; a person without these foundations can only accept whatever the AI produces — including its mistakes.

REASON 02 · Computational thinking is weight training for research

Turning a vague idea into precise, executable steps — decomposing problems, spotting patterns, handling errors — is exactly the skill set of reproducible research. The computer is the most honest teammate you will ever have: if your thinking is fuzzy, the program simply won't work. Train with it long enough and your experiment designs, data analyses, and reports get sharper too.

REASON 03 · The barrier has never been lower — and "can read & judge" has never been scarcer

Ten years ago a cryptic error message could cost you a whole evening. Today you have a 24/7 AI teaching assistant, so the cost of learning the basics is at a historic low. But precisely because everyone can now generate code, generating is worth little — reading, judging, and fixing are the rare skills. And there is one thing AI cannot give you: the moment your first simulation runs and the figure looks exactly the way you predicted — that "I built this" feeling. You'll get it in Module 2 at the latest.

📣 ONE-LINE SUMMARY

"AI can make you stronger, or make you weaker. The choice is yours." — adapted from Kao Chen-Lung (高見龍)

PART 05 · ACTION GUIDE

Five concrete habits before you start

  1. Build the foundation first. Understanding arrays, figures, symbols, algorithms and tables (Modules 1–5) is what lets you judge whether AI-generated scientific code is any good.
  2. Ask "why", not "write it for me". When you meet code you don't understand, ask an AI to explain the logic and decompose the concept — this is exactly the habit Colab Learn Mode (next chapter) is built to train.
  3. Form your own hypothesis before asking. When something breaks, predict the cause first, then bring your hypothesis to the AI — don't just dump the traceback and disengage.
  4. Regularly go AI-free. After each module, redo one exercise with AI switched off. It verifies that you actually learned it — not merely that you can follow along.
  5. Reading your code is a duty, not an option. Before submitting anything, ask yourself: what does this code do, line by line? If you can't answer, don't submit it.
